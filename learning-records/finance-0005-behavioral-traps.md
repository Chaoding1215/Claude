## What Was Covered

- DALBAR QAIB 2023 data: 20-year gap between S&P 500 (9.8%) and average equity investor (6.0%) = 3.8%/yr behavior gap
  - Compounded: $100k → $660k (index) vs $320k (average investor behavior) — half the wealth lost to behavior
- Three core behavioral biases:
  1. **Loss Aversion** (Kahneman & Tversky 1979): losses feel ~2.25x worse than equivalent gains feel good; causes panic selling at bottoms
  2. **Disposition Effect** (Shefrin & Statman 1985; Odean 1998): investors sell winners 50% more often than losers; keeps worst assets, sells best
  3. **Recency Bias**: investors extrapolate recent trends; net inflows peak at market tops, lowest at bottoms
- Interactive loss aversion slider: user inputs required gain to accept 50/50 bet vs $1000 loss → shows their personal ratio
- Three scenario quizzes: panic during crash (loss aversion), which fund to sell (disposition effect), FOMO on trending sector (recency bias)
- Investment Policy Statement (IPS): 6-element template as the systematic defense against all three biases
- Key meta-insight: knowing about biases doesn't make you immune; systems beat willpower

## Non-Obvious Insights to Preserve

- Kahneman himself said he still *feels* loss aversion even knowing about it — this is crucial: the antidote is process, not knowledge
- The "behavior gap" between fund return and investor return (DALBAR) is larger than the gap between active and passive funds — behavior is a bigger cost than fees
- FOMO-driven buying tends to occur exactly when future expected returns are lowest (after prices have already risen) — the peak of recent-bias is the trough of future return
