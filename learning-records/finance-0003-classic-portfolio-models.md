## What Was Covered

Three classic models with full allocations, historical performance, and economic logic:

### 60/40
- 60% global equity + 40% investment-grade bonds
- Most studied; ~9% annualized return, ~11% volatility, max drawdown ~-35%
- Vulnerability: 2022 "stock-bond correlation breakdown" — both fell when rates rose sharply

### All Weather (Ray Dalio, Bridgewater, 1996)
- 30% stocks / 40% long bonds / 15% mid bonds / 7.5% gold / 7.5% commodities
- Based on "risk parity" — equalizing risk contribution across 4 economic environments
- ~7.5% annualized, ~7.5% vol, max drawdown ~-20%, Sharpe ~0.62 (highest of the three)
- Four seasons: Rising/Falling growth × Rising/Falling inflation

### Permanent Portfolio (Harry Browne, 1981)
- 25% each: stocks, long bonds, gold, cash
- Equal allocation across prosperity/recession/inflation/deflation assets
- ~6.5% annualized, ~7% vol, max drawdown ~-20%
- Extreme simplicity is a feature, not a bug

## Non-Obvious Insights to Preserve

- 60/40's "40% bonds" contributes only ~10% of portfolio risk (because bond vol << stock vol). This is counterintuitive and motivates understanding risk-parity.
- The 2022 failure of 60/40 (−16%) is a reminder that historical correlations are not constant — this is called "correlation breakdown" and tends to happen at the worst times (high inflation environments).
- "Higher absolute return" from 100% stocks is only realizable if the investor doesn't panic-sell during drawdowns. Behavioral sustainability is part of the actual expected return calculation.
- Sharpe ratio matters more than raw return for most investors — it measures return per unit of risk, not just return.
