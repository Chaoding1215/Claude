The Threat Landscape:
Who Attacks and Why

In presales, you will regularly face one question in different forms: "Who is trying to attack us, and what do they want?" This lesson gives you a precise, credible answer — and the vocabulary to prove it.

Why this matters for presales
Customers who don't feel threatened don't buy security. Your job is to help them see their real threat profile — not generically, but specifically. "Nation-state actors are unlikely to target you, but ransomware groups explicitly target your industry" is far more persuasive than "cyberattacks are increasing."

Threat Actors — The Five Categories

Every attacker fits into one of five categories. Each has different motivations, capabilities, and targets. Knowing which category applies to a customer's situation shapes the entire solution conversation.

Actor Motivation Capability Typical targets
Nation-states Espionage, sabotage, influence Highest — unlimited resources, custom tools, years of patience Governments, defense, critical infrastructure, tech firms
Organized crime Financial gain High — professional operations, ransomware-as-a-service Any organization that can pay — healthcare, finance, manufacturing
Hacktivists Ideology, publicity, disruption Medium — DDoS, defacement, data leaks Organizations aligned with a political cause they oppose
Insider threats Revenge, financial, ideology, negligence Variable — high access, low technical skill needed Employer's systems, data, IP
Script kiddies Curiosity, notoriety, low-level crime Low — use existing tools, opportunistic Anyone with unpatched systems or weak credentials
Presales framing — threat profiling
Ask the customer: "What industry are you in, and do you handle regulated data or IP worth stealing?" Their answers immediately narrow which threat actors are relevant. A mid-size retailer faces organized crime and opportunists — not nation-states. A defense contractor faces all five.

The MITRE ATT&CK Framework

MITRE ATT&CK is the industry's shared language for describing how attackers operate. Every serious security vendor references it; every SOC team uses it. You must know what it is and why it matters.

ATT&CK organizes attacker behavior into a matrix of Tactics and Techniques:

  • Tactics — the goal of an action (e.g., Initial Access, Persistence, Lateral Movement, Exfiltration). There are 14 enterprise tactics.
  • Techniques — the method used to achieve a tactic (e.g., Phishing, Pass-the-Hash, Living off the Land). There are hundreds of techniques and sub-techniques.
  • Groups — named threat actor groups (e.g., APT29/Cozy Bear = Russia's SVR) with their known TTPs mapped.
Why ATT&CK matters in a sales conversation
When a vendor says "we detect 95% of MITRE ATT&CK techniques," that's a measurable claim about coverage. When a customer's CISO asks "how does your product map to ATT&CK?", they're asking: which attack methods do you actually stop? Being fluent here signals you're talking to them in their language.

The Cyber Kill Chain

Developed by Lockheed Martin in 2011, the Kill Chain model describes the stages every targeted attack moves through. It's slightly older than ATT&CK but remains widely used — especially in enterprise and government conversations.

2

3

4

5

6

7
Act
  1. Reconnaissance — Attacker researches the target: LinkedIn, job postings, DNS records, exposed services.
  2. Weaponization — Creates a deliverable payload: malicious document, exploit kit, infected USB.
  3. Delivery — Sends the weapon to the victim: phishing email, watering hole website, compromised software update.
  4. Exploitation — The payload executes and exploits a vulnerability.
  5. Installation — Malware is installed; the attacker establishes a foothold.
  6. Command & Control (C2) — Malware phones home; attacker remotely controls the compromised system.
  7. Actions on Objectives — The attacker does what they came to do: steal data, encrypt files, destroy systems.
Presales framing — where does your product stop the chain?
Every security product intervenes at one or more stages of the kill chain. Email security stops Delivery. EDR catches Exploitation and Installation. NDR detects C2 traffic. SIEM correlates across all stages. Use this to position multiple products in a platform conversation — "defense in depth across the kill chain."

Essential Vocabulary

APT

Advanced Persistent Threat. A sophisticated, long-running attack — typically nation-state or organized crime — that stays hidden in a network for months or years. "Persistent" is the key word: they're patient.

TTPs

Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures. The specific behaviors and methods an attacker uses. Defenders share TTPs to recognize the same actor across different victims. MITRE ATT&CK catalogs TTPs.

IOC

Indicator of Compromise. Forensic evidence that an attack occurred: a malicious IP address, a hash of a known malware file, a suspicious domain. Threat intel feeds distribute IOCs for blocking.

Zero-day

A vulnerability that is unknown to the software vendor and has no patch. Extremely valuable — nation-states stockpile them. Once disclosed, the "zero days" of exposure before a patch drops is zero.

Ransomware-as-a-Service

Criminal groups sell ransomware toolkits to affiliates on a revenue-share model. This lowers the skill barrier dramatically — even low-skilled attackers can deploy sophisticated ransomware.

Threat Intelligence

Curated, contextualized information about current threats: active campaigns, attacker TTPs, IOCs, and vulnerability exploitability. Feeds into SIEM, SOAR, and firewall blocking rules.

Knowledge Check

A hospital is hit by ransomware that was deployed by a criminal group operating a "ransomware-as-a-service" platform. Which threat actor category best describes the group running the platform?
An attacker sends a targeted phishing email containing a malicious Word document to a CFO. In the Kill Chain, which stage does this email represent?
A customer's CISO mentions "TTPs" when evaluating your threat detection product. What are they asking about?
A security vendor claims their product detects "Command and Control traffic." At which Kill Chain stage does their product intervene?
