MDR:
Managed Detection and Response

The competitive analysis you reviewed identified MDR as the most suitable solution for SME customers — "lightweight, low barrier to entry." This lesson explains exactly what MDR is, why it exists, how it differs from older managed security services, and how to sell it.

The Problem: Most Organizations Can't Run a SOC

A mature internal SOC requires:

  • 24/7/365 analyst coverage (minimum 6–8 FTEs to cover shifts)
  • SIEM licensing ($500K–$2M/year for enterprise)
  • Threat intelligence subscriptions
  • Specialized threat hunting expertise
  • Incident response capability

Total cost: $3–8M per year for a credible internal SOC. This is out of reach for the vast majority of organizations — mid-market, SME, and even many enterprises.

Meanwhile, attackers don't discriminate by company size. Ransomware groups specifically target mid-market organizations because they have money but lack defenses.

The talent crisis compounds the problem
There is a global shortage of approximately 3.5 million cybersecurity professionals (ISC² 2023). Even organizations that can afford SOC staff struggle to hire and retain them. MDR transfers this burden to a specialized provider that can attract talent at scale.

What MDR Is

MDR (Managed Detection and Response) is a security service where a vendor provides 24/7 threat monitoring, detection, and active response on behalf of the customer — using the vendor's own technology, analysts, and threat intelligence.

The critical word is response. MDR is not just monitoring and alerting — the provider takes action to contain threats. This distinguishes it from older managed security services.

The Managed Security Services Family

📡 MSSP (Managed Security Service Provider) — Legacy

What they do: Monitor your security tools (firewall, SIEM) and send you alerts.

What they don't do: Investigate alerts in depth, hunt for hidden threats, or take action to stop attacks.

The problem: You still need internal staff to triage and respond to every alert. You've outsourced monitoring, not outcomes. Alert fatigue follows you into the managed service.

🔍 MDR (Managed Detection and Response) — Current standard

What they do: Provide their own EDR/XDR technology, deploy it in your environment, run 24/7 monitoring with human analysts, investigate every alert, hunt for threats proactively, and take containment actions (isolating an endpoint, blocking a connection) with your approval or autonomously per agreed playbook.

What you get: A measurable MTTD and MTTR SLA. Outcomes, not just alerts.

Key differentiator: Analysts are specialists — they see threats across hundreds of customer environments. Pattern recognition that no single customer's team can match.

🤖 MXDR (Managed XDR) — Emerging

What it adds: Extends MDR across all telemetry sources — endpoint, network, cloud, identity, email — into a unified managed service. Aligns with the XDR platform trend.

Major vendors (Microsoft, CrowdStrike, Palo Alto) now offer MXDR alongside their platforms.

How MDR Works — The Delivery Model

MDR providers typically operate on one of two models:

Technology-led MDR

Provider deploys their own EDR/XDR agent in the customer environment. All telemetry flows to the provider's platform. Examples: CrowdStrike Falcon Complete, SentinelOne Vigilance.

Agnostic MDR

Provider works with the customer's existing security tools. Ingests data from whatever SIEM/EDR is already deployed. More flexible; typically higher integration cost. Examples: Arctic Wolf, Rapid7 MDR.

Market Segmentation — Who Buys MDR

🏢 SME (50–500 employees)

No security team. MDR is their entire security operations function. Price sensitivity is high — look for lightweight, standardized offerings. Highest growth segment.

🏬 Mid-market (500–5,000)

Small internal security team, overwhelmed by alerts. MDR augments them — handles nights and weekends, provides expertise the team lacks. Co-managed model.

🏦 Enterprise (5,000+)

Has a SOC but wants specialized threat hunting, faster response, or coverage during staff transitions. MDR supplements internal capability for specific use cases.

Key MDR Vendors & the OCD Angle

The competitive analysis you reviewed calls out OCD (Orange CyberDefense) as the model to study. OCD's strength is exactly the MDR and managed services layer — they integrate products from Palo Alto, Fortinet, and others, add their own SOC analysts and threat intelligence, and deliver an E2E managed security service.

This is the integrator advantage: OCD doesn't win on products — they win because no single product vendor can deliver the full service layer that customers actually need.

Key Metrics to Know — SLA Language

MDR contracts are defined by SLAs. Know these terms for any commercial conversation:

  • MTTD SLA — e.g., "we detect threats within 1 hour of activity." Premium MDR providers offer sub-hour MTTD.
  • MTTR SLA — e.g., "we contain threats within 4 hours of detection." Response speed is the primary differentiator.
  • Co-managed vs. fully managed — co-managed means the provider acts with the customer's approval; fully managed means autonomous containment per agreed playbook.
  • Coverage scope — which telemetry sources are included: endpoint only? Network? Cloud? Identity? Email? Scope defines price and coverage.
